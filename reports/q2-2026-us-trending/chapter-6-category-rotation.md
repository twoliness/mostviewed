# Category Rotation

## The weekly composition of the US trending head, Q2 2026

*Chapter 6 — Q2 2026 YouTube US Trending Playbook*

---

**In the first week of Q2 (April 13–19), Gaming held 24.2% of the top-25 slots on US YouTube trending. In the third-to-last week (May 18–24), Gaming held 52.9%. Music, which led every week in April with 30–46% share, ceded first place to Gaming on May 11 and never took it back. The Q2 story is a single unbroken trend: Gaming climbed and Music retreated, with a monoculture peak in mid-May that Chapter 5 covers as its own event. This chapter shows the whole quarter's weekly composition.**

## Six things this table says

**1. Q2 has exactly one category rotation event, and it happened the week of May 11.**
Weeks Apr 13 → May 4: Music leads. Weeks May 11 → Jun 22: Gaming leads. The crossover week (May 11) shows Music at 36.6% and Gaming at 40.4% — the first week Gaming surpassed Music. The gap widens through May 18 (Gaming 52.9%, the Gaming Lockout peak documented in Chapter 5) and never fully recovers.

If your Q3 plan is drafted from "what won in Q2," the answer depends materially on which week you sample. Sampling the first half returns "Music dominates." Sampling the second half returns "Gaming dominates." Sampling the quarter as a whole averages to Gaming ~40% / Music ~34% and misses the trend.

**2. Gaming grew from 24% to 46% share over 10 weeks — an 88% relative increase.**
This is a Gaming ascension quarter. The first week of Q2 (24.2% share) and the last recovered week (46.3% share) frame a doubling in relative terms. Chapter 5's Gaming Lockout (May 17–28) sits in the middle of this trend, not outside it. The lockout was Gaming's *peak*, not Gaming's *arrival* — arrival happened around May 11.

**3. Music retreated from 46% to 33% share over the same span.**
Music's decline is the mirror image of Gaming's rise, though less severe. Music never falls below 24% (its lockout-week low). Post-lockout, Music stabilizes around 33–36% share. Music remains the second-largest category every week from May 18 onward.

**4. Entertainment is remarkably stable.**
Entertainment's weekly share ranges from 10.1% (Jun 1) to 15.8% (Apr 13). Across 11 weeks, that's a spread of 5.7 percentage points — narrower than Music or Gaming week-to-week volatility. Entertainment appears to have a structural floor around 10% and ceiling around 15% of the top-25. If your business plan depends on Entertainment being "the largest category," it isn't and wasn't in Q2.

**5. People & Blogs briefly displaced Entertainment as the #3 category in early May.**
Weeks of May 4 and May 11, People & Blogs jumped into the top 3 (9.7% and 8.7% respectively) while Entertainment dropped below the top 3. By May 18 Entertainment returned. The signal isn't strong enough to build strategy on, but it's a real fluctuation and probably tied to specific viral moments in that window.

**6. Four categories account for 87–94% of the top-25 every week.**
Music + Gaming + Entertainment + People & Blogs make up nearly all of the recovered top-25 in every week of Q2. The remaining share ("Other" column) is split across News, Sports, Comedy, Film & Animation, Howto, Science, Autos, Pets, Education, Travel — 10 categories collectively fighting for 6-14% of the chart head.

If your Q3 plan involves categories in the "Other" bucket, you are playing a smaller-pool game. That's a strategic choice with implications: smaller pool means less competition (Chapter 1 showed this — Entertainment Shorts has 425 competitors, People & Blogs Medium has 54) but also less habitual audience overlap with the categories driving discovery.

## What this can't tell you

- **Absolute view counts by category.** This is share-of-chart-head, not share-of-total-views. A category with fewer top-25 slots can still drive more overall views via long-tail content that never touches the top-25.
- **US-market vs. Indian-market composition.** Chapter 2 established that many of the top-of-chart Q2 videos are Indian-market content. This chapter's category share does not decompose to language/region. Both the Music share and the People & Blogs share almost certainly include a significant Indian-market slice.
- **Cause of Gaming's climb.** We can measure the rise; we cannot attribute it to any specific YouTube algorithmic change, product launch, seasonal effect, or content-cycle driver. Chapter 5 speculates about monoculture failure modes for the specific lockout window but does not explain the quarter-long trend.

## What to do about it

**For Q3 planning:** treat Gaming as the plurality category on US YouTube going into Q3 — not the largest by user base or revenue, but the largest by chart share on the surface most agency briefs care about. Music remains a strong secondary format with different economics (Chapters 3 and 4 documented Music's velocity vs. engagement decay profile).

**For content investment:** the "Other" 10 categories share 6–14% of the top-25. That's roughly 1–2 slots per week per category if evenly split. Any Q3 plan built around a specific "Other" category needs to explicitly justify why it will land in that scarce slice.

**For competitive positioning:** if you're advising a creator whose category-of-record dropped share in Q2 (Music being the largest example), the honest advice is that the algorithmic tide moved. Q3 predictions should account for the possibility that the tide keeps moving in the Gaming direction rather than reverting to a Music-led equilibrium.

## Methodology

- **Source:** `video_rank_history` rows where `chart = 'global:videos'` AND `rank ≤ 25` AND `captured_at` in `[2026-04-15, 2026-06-27)`.
- **Weekly aggregation:** SQLite `DATE(captured_at, 'weekday 0', '-6 days')` groups by Monday-anchored week.
- **Category assignment:** joined via `videos.category_id`. YouTube's own category tag on the video (not derived).
- **Share formula:** `SUM(rows) FILTER (WHERE category_id = X)` ÷ `SUM(rows)` per week. Multiple ticks of the same video count as multiple rows — a video holding rank 5 across 20 ticks contributes 20 to its category's numerator that week.
- **Recovery caveat:** week of May 18 (the Gaming Lockout) had 46% of its bucketed 30-minute snapshots unrecoverable under default reconstruction parameters. Recovery under aggressive parameters brought this to 54%. Share numbers for the May 18 row are calculated over the recovered slice only; the underlying Gaming share was almost certainly higher than 52.9% — direct sampling of raw buckets showed Gaming shares of 62–75% in that window (see Chapter 5). Other weeks are >95% recovered.
- **Sample gate:** every week in the table has ≥ 1,000 top-25 rank rows.
